"Is the looksmaxxer a morality tale for our time—a sort of Hogarthian parable about everything, or at least some of the main things, that ail us as a society in 2026? … There's one, for example, that situates it at a biological level, as a kind of morbid symptom of prosperity and the aimlessness that comes with it. Deep Dive
Turkey's S-400 Off-Ramp
Ankara wants the F-35 back. Washington isn't sure it wants Ankara to have it. Three vantage points on the same standoff.

Turkey's Cooperation with Russia and China Is a Major Reason It Shouldn't Receive the F-35 Jet [more · less · never again]

Sinan Ciddi, FDD / The Algemeiner · ~6 min

"President Trump departed the NATO Summit in Ankara without announcing Turkey's reinstatement in the F-35 program, a major disappointment to Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdogan. This omission is significant because it highlights the broader strategic issue at stake. … Trump's decision should not be seen as a diplomatic slight; it is sound policy. Turkey was removed from the F-35 program in 2019 after taking delivery of Russia's S-400 air-defense system. The Pentagon warned at the time that Ankara could not possess both the S-400 and the F-35, because the Russian system could collect sensitive data on the aircraft's stealth capabilities."

Selling the S-400 to Qatar or the UAE Solves Turkey's Problem, Not Washington's [more · less · never again]

Sinan Ciddi & Tyler Stapleton, FDD · ~7 min

"Turkey's decade-long standoff with Washington over the S-400 may finally be nearing an inflection point, though not in the tidy way Ankara once imagined, and not without a fresh dose of frustration from the NATO summit in Ankara. … Trump left the July 2026 NATO summit … without announcing Turkey's reinstatement to the F-35 program … Instead, Trump told reporters he hadn't 'made up his mind,' leaving the existing prohibition in place, while Erdogan gave only an evasive response to Turkish reporters pressing him on the matter. The gap between expectation and outcome may have been the kind of pressure that pushed Ankara toward a workaround — one designed to hand Washington a legal off-ramp rather than wait indefinitely for a political decision that hasn't materialized."

From the Field

Commander's Intent [more · less · never again] & Place and Purpose [more · less · never again]

Bruce Ivar Gudmundsson, The Tactical Notebook · ~12 min combined

"We must always remember that fruitful cooperation among companies in a battalion, among battalions in a regiment, and so on, becomes nearly impossible when the units receive orders that have not been thought through from A to Z. … In war, however, we should issue orders as if the messenger carrying a revision will not be able to reach his destination. For that reason, orders sent to subordinate commanders at the outset of battle must be framed so that they remain valid, at least in spirit, as the fight develops."

"It would be wrong, however, for the battalion commander to assign a specific spot, even when features of the terrain seem to invite it. The company commander will find the right place, a position that lets him fulfill his mission. … If he orders, 'The 10th Company will occupy the grove to the right front of the Windmill Hill and cover the battery,' he overshoots the mark. He has taken the choice of means out of the company commander's hands."

The Davis Wing, the B-24 Liberator, and the Self-Made Bet That Paid Off [more · less · never again]

War on the Rocks · ~8 min

"The most produced American military aircraft of World War II was not a small fighter, a trainer, or a simple utility airplane — it was a complex four-engine heavy bomber called the B-24 Liberator. Over the course of five years, American industry produced a staggering 18,482 of these bombers, and they served in every theater of the war. The B-24 was a workhorse with strategic impact, but it would not have had its utility — or possibly even existed — were it not for its wing."

Celebrating Bunny Lang

NYRB Poets, via New York Review Books newsletter · ~6 min

"'You probably haven't heard of Bunny Lang,' begins Rosa Campbell in her introduction to The Miraculous Season: Selected Poems by Bunny Lang. 'Or, if you have, it's because you're a Frank O'Hara fan.' Considered a marginal figure in the decades following her death at just thirty-two, Lang has largely been regarded in the light of her more famous contemporaries … 'Lang was the fiery, frantic core of the Boston and Cambridge literary scene: a poet, a playwright, an actress, a director and, according to Susan Howe, "a Valkyrie."' This arts scene included John Ashbery, Alison Lurie, Edward Gorey, Mary Manning Howe, and Robert Bly, with Lang as a central figure in the group, which Bly once called 'an astonishing collection of intense maniacs.'"
