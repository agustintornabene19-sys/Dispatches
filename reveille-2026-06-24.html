THE LONG READS

War by Other Means

JOHN SEVERINI · PALLADIUM

"In December 2024, the Ukrainian National Guard's 13th Khartiia Brigade carried out a combined ground and air assault near the town of Lyptsi. Air assets coordinated with dispersed ground forces bounding from cover to cover. Dozens of attackers overran Russian positions, cleared mines in the vicinity, and laid down a defensive perimeter around the captured territory. But not a single Ukrainian was present on the battlefield."

"The growing use of drones is both a cause and a consequence of an ongoing transformation of military power… this means that a fundamental source of political power has begun to shift from the people toward the firms that make those machines. This is leading to an erosion of the popular constraints that have historically disciplined the use of force, and an ever-greater insulation of the state from revolutions and popular uprisings."

"On its face, this trend seems humane — fewer soldiers, fewer deaths — but it also risks changing the political costs of war, which becomes more palatable, even routine. 'Forever wars' emerge from such a disjunction between military effort and political consequence."

Childhood Freedom Is Now a Luxury Good

JOHANN KURTZ · BECOMING NOBLE

"Giving children the freedom they need to develop agency is now a luxury good. The number of neighborhoods in which it is normal for children to leave the house and roam all day has collapsed… This is a curious inversion of an older pattern. For most of history the peasant's son had the run of the village while the noble's son was kept under tutors. Now it is the wealthy child who is sent out to enjoy the freedom and adventure of camps and screenless schools, while working and underclass children are kept indoors and screened up."

"In 1971 the Policy Studies Institute in London surveyed how English children moved about. Roughly eighty per cent of seven- and eight-year-olds were allowed to travel to school without an adult. By 1990… that share had fallen to about nine per cent. Inside a single generation an everyday liberty had disappeared. The obvious explanation is that the world grew more dangerous… But it did not. Violent crime in the United States fell by half from the early 1990s, across the decades in which supervision tightened hardest."

Toward Ethical Populism

ERIC KAUFMANN · FIRST THINGS

Eric Kaufmann lays out a roadmap for conservative politics after the Trump era — a fusion of populism's genuine goods with a moral system that abandons Trump's faults, embracing both "Thatcherite disruption" and "Scrutonian order."

"Conservatives must learn from Trump without inheriting his pathologies. We need executive action to remove the rot, but also require cultural legitimation to win the long-term battle of ideas."

Pair it with Jacob Williams in Postliberal Order, who frames the same moment more radically: "Liberalism is collapsing. What, exactly, will emerge from its ashes? Across the world, the most vibrant political projects and the most inspiring political ideas are those that recognize the palpable exhaustion of the liberal, freedom-maximizing paradigm." FROM THE BRANCH

Field Gun Batteries

THE TACTICAL NOTEBOOK

"On 2 August 1914, the 47ème Régiment d'Artillerie de Campagne (47th Field Artillery) began the process of mobilization. With the aid of recalled reservists, requisitioned horses, and equipment brought out of storage, it brought existing units up to war strength and, soon thereafter, began to form new units of various sorts."

A redleg's reminder that mobilization is its own discipline: the senior batteries drew active-duty gunners and young reservists, while the batteries de renforcement ran on older men "strolling on civvie street for a longer period of time" — the same triage every artillery regiment still faces when it scales for war.
