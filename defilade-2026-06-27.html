The Culture War

Stop Talking About Mental Health
Ashley Frawley · Compact · ~12 min
A polemic against the therapeutic state: the more we are urged to "talk about" mental health, the sicker the numbers get.
"On April 30, 2026, the OECD published a new report claiming that more than one in five people across OECD and EU countries experience a mental disorder, making mental ill health 'one of the most significant public health and economic challenges' of our time… The truth is that we need to stop talking about mental health. Everyone who tells us we need to 'talk more' about mental health says it like it's something entirely new, as if Philip Rieff didn't already declare the Triumph of the Therapeutic back in 1966."
Childhood Freedom Is Now a Luxury Good
Johann Kurtz · Becoming Noble · ~11 min
The free-range childhood has not vanished so much as concentrated — into a guarded privilege of the few.
"Giving children the freedom they need to develop agency is now a luxury good. The number of neighborhoods in which it is normal for children to leave the house and roam all day has collapsed… This is a curious inversion of an older pattern. For most of history the peasant's son had the run of the village while the noble's son was kept under tutors. Now it is the wealthy child who is sent out to enjoy the freedom and adventure of camps and screenless schools, while working and underclass children are kept indoors and screened up."

The Right Argues With Itself

A paired argument: the folkish case against the Axial/Christian inheritance — and a reported counterpoint on what happens when "dissident" identity politics curdles.
Turning Our Back on Tradition
Imperium Press · ~16 min
"For almost all of recorded history, Axial theologies have staged themselves as defenders of authority and tradition, much as modern-day conservatives do. But beneath the surface, a deeper foundation lies concealed. There was something that these theologies once replaced—they were once the revolutionaries, the assailants against all authority and tradition, and they bear the ineradicable mark of their revolutionary past. A leopard never changes its spots, and anti-authoritarianism does not become authority with the 'silent lapse of time.'"

Surya Gowda · The Dispatch · ~14 min · the counterpoint
Quoted briefly because the piece sits behind a paywall: Gowda interviews Richard Hanania and other former movement insiders on why the dissident-identitarian right keeps selecting for its dumbest members.
"What happens when a former white nationalist realizes his old movement is getting dumber and dumber? Dispatch reporter Surya Gowda talks to Richard Hanania and other former members of the white nationalist movement about what drew them to the ideology—and what ultimately led them out." Identity-based movements, as one source puts it, are "the bat signal for every loser."

Britain & Europe

The British Public Is to Blame for the U.K.'s Dysfunction
Charles C. W. Cooke · National Review · ~12 min
With Starmer gone, seven prime ministers in ten years. Cooke argues the rot is downstream of the electorate, not just "the system."
"Keir Starmer has fallen. Long live Andy Burnham! Or Angela Rayner or Wes Streeting, or Yvette Cooper, or whichever other poor, downtrodden sod manages to finagle the job of prime minister of Great Britain and Northern Ireland for a few sweaty and bruising months before this interminable game of political hot potato chooses a new victim… At this point, taking the reins of the British government is a little like becoming the drummer in Spinal Tap. You know that you're going to be toast pretty soon; the only interesting question is how."

Washington Held an Unprecedented Upper Hand Over Iran — Then Cashed It In
Janatan Sayeh · FDD / The Jerusalem Report · ~12 min · the hawk
"The odds had never been worse for the Islamic Republic than in early June 2026. Its missile infrastructure had been devastated, senior military leaders killed, nuclear facilities bombed, the repression apparatus weakened, oil exports reduced to zero, and inflation driven to its highest level since World War II… Yet, whether the newly announced Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) converts that upper hand into concessions, or cashes it in prematurely, is another matter. From the Field

War by Other Means
John Severini · Palladium · ~17 min
Robotic warfare is shifting the source of state power from citizens to firms — and with it, the social contract.
"In December 2024, the Ukrainian National Guard's 13th Khartiia Brigade carried out a combined ground and air assault near the town of Lyptsi… Dozens of attackers overran Russian positions, cleared mines in the vicinity, and laid down a defensive perimeter around the captured territory. But not a single Ukrainian was present on the battlefield… A fundamental source of political power has begun to shift from the people toward the firms that make those machines."
Virtual Fronts, Mobile Firepower
Bazaar of War · ~14 min
"The growth of armies between the 18th and 20th centuries profoundly changed the nature of operational art… By exploiting geography, manmade defenses, and mobility, an army could defend a much wider area than its numbers alone would suggest. This could give rise to what I call a virtual front: a semi-stable dynamic that resembles the operational patterns of an extended front, even while looking completely different in all the particulars."
The Artillery Park of an Army Corps
The Tactical Notebook · ~9 min · redleg's corner
"While assembling hundreds of men, horses, and vehicles into ammunition columns, the depot of the 47th Field Artillery Regiment also completed the modest task of forming the headquarters for the '2nd Echelon of the Artillery park of the 7th Army Corps'… Composed of six officers, five sergeants, and fifteen cannoneers, this little outfit provided an army corps with an organizational home for half of its ammunition columns."

Aesthetics & the Good Life

Anti-Bourdain
Raw Egg Nationalist · ~13 min
A heretical reading of the patron saint of foodie cosmopolitanism — conformity sold as rebellion.
"Anthony Bourdain. The Unabomber Ted Kaczynski never knew of him, I'd guess, but he understood his type well. Bourdain was an oversocialised leftist. A man who, to avoid a deep sense of guilt and worthlessness, overidentifies with the system and presents conformity with its values as a kind of rebellion… Everything Bourdain sold as edgy and punk… was indistinguishable from the spirit of our age."

What JD Vance Found in the Church
Robert Barron · First Things · reviewing JD Vance, Communion: Finding My Way Back to Faith · ~14 min
Bishop Barron sees an Augustinian motivation at the heart of Vance's account of his conversion: "Though his lust for success had brought him to the heights, he realized he didn't particularly like the law and certainly didn't want to practice it. He was a success addict, or rather, an approval addict, and his heart was still utterly restless. It was this emptiness that opened him to a reconsideration of the Christianity of his youth."
